[diabetes]
Diabetes is a chronic metabolic condition characterized by elevated blood glucose (hyperglycemia).
Type 2 diabetes is the most common form and is strongly associated with obesity, sedentary lifestyle, and genetics.
Key risk indicators: high fasting glucose (>126 mg/dL), elevated HbA1c (>6.5%), high BMI (>30), increased age, and family history.
Glucose is the most important single predictor of diabetes risk.
A DiabetesPedigreeFunction value above 0.5 indicates significant hereditary risk.
BMI above 30 substantially increases diabetes risk; BMI above 35 is considered high risk.
Insulin resistance, reflected by high insulin levels with elevated glucose, is a hallmark of pre-diabetes.
Pregnancy-related glucose intolerance (gestational diabetes) raises lifetime diabetes risk.
Lifestyle interventions (diet, exercise) can delay or prevent Type 2 diabetes in high-risk individuals.
Uncontrolled diabetes leads to complications including neuropathy, retinopathy, nephropathy, and cardiovascular disease.

[heart]
Cardiovascular disease (CVD) is the leading cause of death worldwide.
Coronary artery disease is caused by atherosclerosis — plaque buildup narrowing the coronary arteries.
Key risk factors: age, male sex, hypertension (trestbps > 130 mmHg), high cholesterol (chol > 240 mg/dL), diabetes, smoking, and family history.
Chest pain type (cp): 0=typical angina, 1=atypical angina, 2=non-anginal, 3=asymptomatic; asymptomatic chest pain in older patients is a red flag.
Low maximum heart rate during exercise (thalach < 120 bpm) suggests poor cardiac reserve.
Exercise-induced angina (exang=1) strongly indicates coronary artery disease.
Elevated ST depression (oldpeak > 2.0) is a significant marker of myocardial ischemia.
Number of major vessels (ca=0-3) colored by fluoroscopy correlates with disease severity.
Thalassemia type 2 (reversible defect) and type 3 (fixed defect) indicate prior or ongoing cardiac ischemia.
High cholesterol, elevated blood pressure, and diabetes together multiply cardiovascular risk exponentially.

[kidney]
Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) involves progressive loss of kidney function over months or years.
CKD affects approximately 10% of the global population and is often detected late due to lack of early symptoms.
Key diagnostic markers: elevated serum creatinine (sc > 1.2 mg/dL), reduced hemoglobin (hemo < 12 g/dL), high blood urea (bu > 40 mg/dL), and low specific gravity (sg < 1.010).
Albuminuria (al > 0) is one of the earliest signs of kidney damage.
Hypertension (htn=1) is both a cause and consequence of CKD and accelerates its progression.
Diabetes mellitus (dm=1) is the leading cause of CKD worldwide.
Low packed cell volume (pcv < 30%) indicates anemia, a common CKD complication.
Elevated potassium (pot > 5.5 mEq/L) and low sodium (sod < 135 mEq/L) indicate electrolyte imbalance in advanced CKD.
Anemia in CKD results from reduced erythropoietin production by damaged kidneys.
CKD stages 3-5 (GFR < 60 mL/min) require specialist nephrology care and possible dialysis preparation.

[liver]
Liver disease encompasses a wide range of conditions including fatty liver, hepatitis, cirrhosis, and liver cancer.
The liver performs over 500 vital functions including detoxification, protein synthesis, and bile production.
Key diagnostic enzymes: elevated ALT (Alamine_Aminotransferase > 40 IU/L) and AST (Aspartate_Aminotransferase > 40 IU/L) indicate hepatocyte damage.
Total Bilirubin > 1.2 mg/dL and Direct Bilirubin > 0.3 mg/dL indicate impaired bile excretion or hemolysis.
Elevated Alkaline Phosphotase (> 120 IU/L) suggests bile duct obstruction or bone disease.
Low albumin (< 3.5 g/dL) indicates reduced liver synthetic function and is a marker of chronic liver disease severity.
Low albumin-to-globulin ratio (< 1.0) suggests chronic liver disease or inflammation.
Alcoholic liver disease, non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD), and viral hepatitis (B and C) are the most common causes.
AST/ALT ratio > 2 is characteristic of alcoholic liver disease; ratio < 1 is more typical of viral hepatitis.
Liver fibrosis and cirrhosis are irreversible; early detection and lifestyle changes are critical.
